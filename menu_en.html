Let's start with a bit of history





In the year 1893, just outside the San Juan de Dios temple, next to the entrance, Mrs. Micaela Aquino (1st Grandmother) set up a small stand to sell hot chocolate and enchiladas to all the people who came to the market, or "plaza day", which took place every Saturday in the Verde Antequera.


That was the name once given to the city of Oaxaca. Alongside her was her only daughter, who sold “margarita” pottery (clay) made in the workshop she had inherited from her father, Narciso López, recently deceased. She had taken charge of the family business.


After Mrs. Micaela passed away, her daughter, Apolonia López Aquino (2nd Grandmother), took over the chocolate, enchiladas, black mole, yellow mole, green mole, and stuffed chile stand inside the Benito Juárez Maza Market, continuing the family tradition. It was there that the food stall name “La Abuelita” was born.


The name was suggested by Professor Arcelia Yañiz, a journalist and historian of the city, who would come every Sunday to have breakfast at the spot already assigned to Mrs. Apolonia inside the Benito Juárez market. One day she said: “Hey, Pola (a nickname), what’s your business called?” Apolonia replied, “I haven’t named it yet.” Arcelia answered, “Why don’t you call it *La Abuelita*? After all, you already have gray hair and grandchildren!” Hahahahaha.


And that’s how the name was born.


Later, when the 20 de Noviembre Market was built, they were assigned three spaces, setting up in stalls number 1, 2, and 3 — which continue to this day.


Mrs. Apolonia had only two sons: Gonzalo Victoria López and Paulino Flores López, who both started out as barbers. Gonzalo, the eldest, together with his wife Mrs. Dina Rodríguez Piña (3rd Grandmother), helped give the business more visibility. After Doña Pola passed away, Gonzalo and his wife inherited the food stand. Three years later, Don Chalo fell ill, and Mrs. Dina, together with all her children, tried to preserve the tradition taught to her by her mother-in-law.


In 2007, Doña Dina passed away, leaving the business to 7 of her 11 children (four had already passed away: Abgelina, Ricardo, Misael, and Bertin, all surnamed Victoria Rodríguez).


From then on, Magdalena Virgen Victoria Rodríguez — affectionately known as Mavi (4th Grandmother) — along with Fili, Gaudencio, Elías, and Dino Marco, took over. To this day, along with the 5th generation — nephews Luis Ángel Victoria Pinacho, Carlos Bertín Victoria Ibañez, and all the younger children now working and organized — they run the **Comedor Típico La Abuelita**, keeping alive the legacy of traditional Oaxacan cuisine and aiming to pass on a lasting tradition to future generations.
